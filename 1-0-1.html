The number line can be split into two regions.
The numbers to the left of 0 are called negative numbers.
The numbers to the right of 0 are called positive numbers.

When we add positive and negative numbers together we are really just moving left or right along the number line.

EXAMPLE: -2 + 5

This expression means "start at position -2 and then move RIGHT 5 units."

If we follow these directions we find ourselves at position +3
Therefore: -2 + 5 = 3

EXAMPLE: -1 - 3

This expression means "start at position -1 and then move left 3 units."

If we follow these directions we find ourselves at position -4.
Therefore: -1 - 3 = -4

Sometimes we find ourselves getting "mixed messages" because more than one symbol is being used. When this happens, are we supposed to go left or right? The first thing we need to do is combine symbols using the following rules:

OPPOSITE symbols become a NEGATIVE
MATCHING symbols become a POSITIVE

Why does this rule work? Let's make a list of all possible combinations. The first symbol gets the phrase "is" for positives and "is not" for negatives. Notice if you read the sentences that form, it predicts the combination.

EXAMPLE: 5 + (-8)

The symbols in the middle(+ and -) are OPPOSITE symbols, and so I can replace them with a NEGATIVE.

5 + (-8) = 5- 8

This expression means "start at position 5 and move LEFT 8 units."

If we follow these instructions we find ourselves at position -3.
Therefore: 5 + (-8) = -3

EXAMPLE: -2 - (-6)

The symbols in the middle (- and -) are MATCHING symbols, and so I can replace them with a POSITIVE.

Therefore: -2 -(-6) = - 2+ 6

This expression means "start at negative 2 and move RIGHT 6 units."

If we follow these instructions we find ourselves at position +4.
Therefore: -2 - (-6) = 4


Multiplying positive and negative numbers is easy if you just remember the following rules.

Multiplying numbers with MATCHING signs gives us a POSITIVE answer.
Multiplying numbers with OPPOSITE signs gives us a NEGATIVE answer.

Why does this rule work? Let's make a list of all possible combinations. The first symbol gets the phrase "is" for positives and "is not" for negatives. Notice if you read the sentences that form, it predicts the combination.

EXAMPLE: -2 \cdot 5

These numbers have opposite signs. (The 2 is negative and the 5 is positive.) Therefore our answer will be NEGATIVE.

-2 \cdot 5 = -10

EXAMPLE: -4 \cdot -10

These numbers have the same signs. (The 4 is negative and the 10 is negative.) Therefore our answer will be POSITIVE.

-4 \cdot -10 = 40


Dividing follows the same rules as multiplication.

Dividing numbers with MATCHING signs gives us a POSITIVE answer.
Dividing numbers with OPPOSITE signs gives us a NEGATIVE answer.

EXAMPLE: \frac{12}{-4}

These numbers have opposite signs. (The 12 is positive and the 4 is negative.) Therefore our answer will be NEGATIVE.

\frac{12}{-4}=-3

EXAMPLE: \frac{-100}{-25}

These numbers have the same signs. (The 100 is negative and the 25 is negative.) Therefore our answer will be POSITIVE.

\frac{-100}{-25} = 4


If an exponent has a negative base then you must pay attention to whether or not it has been placed in parenthesis.

-5^2 is NOT the same as (-5)^2

Because the negative in -5^2 is not in a parenthesis, it is not included in the square. It just waits out front for our exponent to be done.
-5^2 = -(5\cdot5)
= -25

Because the negative in (-5)^2 is in the parenthesis, it is included as part of our square.
(-5)^2 = (-5\cdot-5)
= 25

Notice that our first answer was negative and our second answer was positive. This distinction matters, and it can have a huge influence on the final solution of a multi-step problem.


When evaluating a math expression, we do NOT just interpret each operation in the order it was written. Instead, some operations are considered more important than others, and so we do them first. You may have seen the term PEMDAS or heard the phrase "Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally". This is to help us remember the correct order to use when evaluating a math expression.

For now, let's just focus on one important idea.
Always multiply and divide BEFORE you add or subtract.

EXAMPLE: 10 - 2 \cdot 3

Even though 2 \cdot 3 is written at the END of this expression, we are going to do it first, because multiplying is done BEFORE subtraction.

10 - 2\cdot 3 = 10 - 6
= 4

EXAMPLE: 3 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 4

First we do both multiplications. Then we add.

3 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 4 = 15 + 8
= 23


Now we are going to look at the rest of the Order of Operations.

If an expression has been placed inside a parenthesis, then it means you should do it first, no matter what. Similarly, if there is an exponent, then you should apply the exponent before you simplify any further.

EXAMPLE: 5\cdot(8-2)

We usually save subtraction until the end. But because it is inside a parenthesis, we will do it first.

5\cdot(8-2) = 5 \cdot (6)
=30

EXAMPLE: 2 \cdot 3^2

Before we multiply the 2 \cdot 3 we need to deal with the exponent.

2 \cdot 3^2 = 2 \cdot 9
=18
