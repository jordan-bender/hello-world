If A \div B = a whole number (NOT a decimal) then we say that B is a factor of A.

EXAMPLE: 12 \div 2 = 6     because we got whole number answer, 2 is a factor of 12.

EXAMPLE: 12 \div 5 = 2.4     because we got a decimal answer, 5 is NOT a factor of 12.

Some numbers have lots of factors

EXAMPLE: 24 can be divided by 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24

Some numbers have very few factors.

EXAMPLE: 7 can be divided by 1, 7

If a number can only be divided by 1 and itself we call it a PRIME NUMBER.

EXAMPLE: 8 can be divided by 1, 2, 4, 8 therefore it is NOT a prime number

EXAMPLE: 13 can be divided by 1, 13 therefore it IS a prime number

Finding the factors of a number is a lot easier to do if you know your times tables. However, with a calculator, you can also check for factors by using trial and error.

EXAMPLE: What are the factors of 30?

30 \div 3 = 10     therefore both 3 and 10 are factors of 30

Prime numbers are the building blocks that all integers on the number line are built from. For example, you can build the number 12 by multiplying the prime number 3 by the prime number 4.

EXAMPLE: 12 = 3 \cdot 4

Just as every human has unique DNA, every integer on the number line has a unique set of prime building blocks. Breaking numbers down into their prime building blocks can be useful. When we do this, it is called a “Prime Factor Tree.” The name comes from the fact that when drawn out on paper, the process looks like tree branches.

EXAMPLE: Express the number 36 as the product of prime factors.

If you know your times tables, then you know that 36 = 4 \cdot 9

We can draw this as two branches splitting out of 36.

Next we ask ourselves, "Is the number 4 a prime number?" The answer is no.
"Is the number 9 a prime number?" The answer is no.
This means that both factors need to be broken down further.
In this case, 4 = 2 \cdot 2 and 9 = 3 \cdot 3.
We can draw this as more branches splitting apart.

Next we ask ourselves, "Is the number 2 a prime number?" The answer is yes.
"Is the number 3 a prime number?" The answer is yes.
It helps to circle prime numbers to visually show you have reached the end of the branch.

Once you have reached the end of every branch, you can write your final answer. We usually write the answer in ascending order, meaning we start with small factors and end with large factors.

The number 36 can be written as 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 3

This is a unique combination of prime factors. No other number on the number line has the same combination. It is the "number DNA" of 36.

Sometimes two numbers share common factors. Identifying what these factors are can help us simplify our answers.

EXAMPLE: Both 30 and 40 can be divided by the numbers 2, 5, and 10.

The largest factor that two numbers share is called the Greatest Common Factor." In the example shown above, the number 10 was the greatest common factor.

There are two ways to identify the greatest common factor.

Method 1: List all factors of both numbers
Find the largest factor both numbers share.

Method 2: Make a prime factor tree.
Identify the factors which both trees share and multiply them

Let's try it.

What is the greatest common factor of 60 and 72?

Method 1: The factors of 60 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 20, 30, 60
The factors of 72 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 36, 72
The largest factor that both numbers share is 12

Method 2: The prime factor tree for 60 is: 2*2*3*5
The prime factor tree for 72 is: 2*2*2*3*3
The common factors of both trees are 2*2*3
This means the greatest common factor is 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 12

We have learned to identify which factors are shared by two expressions. But sometimes we want to go the other direction. We start with two known factors and we want to know what multiples they both agree on.

For example, the factors 2 and 5 will both go into 10 and 20 and 30.
We are usually interested in the smallest answer. It is called the LEAST COMMON MULTIPLE.
In this case, the least common multiple of 2 and 5 is 10.

There are two ways to identify the least common multiple.

Method 1: List several multiples of both factors.
Look for the smallest multiple they share.

Method 2: Make prime factor trees of both multiples.
Multiply their common factors by their different factors.

Let's try both.

What is the least common multiple of 12 and 20?

Method 1: List multiples of 12:    12, 24, 36, 48, 60
List multples of 20:    20, 40, 60, 80, 100
The smalles multiple they share is 60

Method 2: Factor tree of 12:     2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3
Factor tree of 20:     2 \cdot 2 \cdot 5
Common factors they agree on:    2*2
Different factors they do NOT agree on:    3*5
The least common multiple is    2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 = 60
